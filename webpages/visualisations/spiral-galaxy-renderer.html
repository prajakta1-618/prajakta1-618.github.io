Orbit Geometry

Spiral galaxies rotate, and the angular velocity of rotation decreases with radius. Due to this differential rotation, the arms of spiral galaxies would become more and more tightly wound and become indistinguishable from the rest of the galaxy after only a few orbits (the winding problem). Lin and Shu proposed the density wave model to solve this problem by proposing that the arms are not fixed matter but a spiral-shaped pattern of higher density that stars move through on their orbits. As the galaxy, stars, gas, dust, and other components of the galaxy move through the density wave, the density of the materials increase in the middle of it, while the wave itself does not move a significant amount in comparison to the material. Now the easiest way to model these spiral arms is by using elliptical orbits that are tilted slightly as their radii grow. This is a huge simplification of the orbits, but it gets the visual shape pretty much right. In the following diagram, each purple orbit is one star's elliptical orbit. We tilt each orbit progressively to make the spiral arms emerge.

Add Stars!

We now add material into these orbits. White dots below show individual stars sampled from the de Vaucouleurs bulge surface brightness profile and Freeman disk brightness profile. The bulge is more centrally concentrated and has a steeper brightness drop-off, while the disk is more extended with a shallower brightness gradient. Nebulous blobs are drawn with additive blending — they represent unresolved stellar populations and diffuse emission. These blobs accumulate brightness in dense arm regions, revealing the spiral structure as a continuous glow.

Make it rotate!

After assembling the galaxy, we now make it rotate. Each star orbits at its own angular velocity ω(r) globally following the flat galaxy rotation curve that arises because of dark matter. Differential rotation would destroy material arms in just a couple of rotations, but the density-wave arms persist because they are a matter density perturbation wave, not fixed matter.

Trailing arms — arms curve away from the rotation direction. A large majority of observed spiral galaxies have trailing arms. They are stable because the Coriolis force damps any perturbation that would make them lead.
